Dimensions of market structure

Market Structure (Krugman and Wells).

Perfect competition

  • Price-Taking - A producer or consumer who does not have enough influence on the market to affect prices. A price-taking producer must set their price at or below the market price. A price-taking consumer must offer a price at least as high as the market price.

If both consumers and producers are price-taking, we say the market is perfectly competitive.

If just producers are price-takers, it is a perfectly competitive industry.

Perfect competition requirements

  • There must be enough producers and no producer can have a large enough market share to affect prices. (E.g. corner stores and gas stations)

(market share is the fraction of total revenue for the industry made up from a particular firms revenues.)

  • Consumers must regard the good from all firms as equivalent to each other. Goods are perfect substitutes. (E.g. oranges or other fruits, perhaps pens, bread.)

  • Free entry and exit of firms (other firms cant keep firms out). (E.g. any business that requires licensing is not a competitive market.)

Monopoly

  • One firm produces the good.
  • Since there is one firm, that firm can set the price.

Monopoly requirements

A monopolist must keep other firms from entering the market:

  • Control natural resources (DeBeers diamonds).
  • Technological superiority (Other firms cant produce).
  • Increasing returns to scale (they can produce more cheaply).
  • Government barriers: licenses, copyright, patents

Oligopoly

  • An oligopoly is a few firms that have some price-setting power. Oligopolist firms can set prices to some extent, can collude to reduce production and/or elevate prices, and otherwise extort additional profits from consumers.

  • The typical oligopoly game theory problem is the prisoners dilemma. Do firms collude and keep output low and prices high? Or do they cheat?

Measuring market concentration

The Herfindal-Hirschman Index (HHI) measures market concentration, and is the sum of squared market shares. For example, if Amazon, Wal-Mart, Target, and five other smaller competitors have market shares of \(25%\), \(45%\), \(15%\), and \(5%\) each, the HHI is

\[HHI = 25^2 + 45^2 + 15^2 + 5 \times 5^2 = 3,000.\]

Monopolistic Competition

All of the previous markets assume goods that are indistinquishable to consumers.

But what if goods are distinguishable, or at least seem distinguisable?

Then firms engage in monopolistic competition.

  • Monopolistic because each firm produces a slightly different product.
  • Competition because consumers still substitute between similar goods, so firms compete on price and quality.
